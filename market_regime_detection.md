# Market Regime Detection: State of the Art and a Prioritized Upgrade Path

A research synthesis for a personal SPY swing-trading system that currently uses ADX(14) + SMA(200) to label SPY as `trending_up / trending_down / choppy / transitioning`.

This is opinionated. Claims that are widely believed but weakly supported are flagged as such. Claims that are unfashionable but empirically robust are also flagged. The goal is to tell you what's worth your weekend, not to be encyclopedic.

---

## TL;DR — The bottom line

1. **Your current setup isn't crazy, but it's a 1990s technical-analysis taxonomy attached to two lagging indicators.** The trichotomy `trending / choppy / transitioning` is not a formal regime taxonomy in any quant literature I can find. It comes from the technical-analysis tradition (Wilder/Chande), not from Hamilton (1989), Ang & Bekaert (2002), or any modern quant work. Practitioners and academics define regimes by *latent statistical states inferred from data* (HMMs, change-point models) or by *cross-asset behavior* (risk-on/off, growth-inflation quadrants), not by ADX bands.

2. **ADX + SMA(200) captures one axis (trend) and ignores the axis that actually matters most for swing risk: volatility.** Modern regime work consistently identifies volatility as the dominant, most-tradeable, and most-persistent regime variable. VIX clusters; ADX does not.

3. **The single highest-leverage upgrade is to add a volatility regime layer (VIX absolute level + VIX vs. its own trend) and tie it to position sizing rather than to a binary on/off filter.** This is free, takes an afternoon, and is supported by both the academic and practitioner literatures.

4. **The second-highest-leverage upgrade is to add a credit-stress signal (HY OAS from FRED).** Credit spreads lead equity drawdowns by 1–3 days and are the single most widely-cited "real" regime signal in cross-asset research. Free via [FRED `BAMLH0A0HYM2`](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/BAMLH0A0HYM2).

5. **A 2-state Gaussian HMM on (log-return, realized vol) is the right "next step" if you want to graduate from rule-based to model-based regime classification.** It's standard, available in `hmmlearn`, and gives you a probability instead of a hard label — which is what you actually want for soft position sizing. *But only if you walk-forward-fit it correctly.* Smoothed HMM probabilities on a single full-history fit are the #1 way people accidentally publish look-ahead-biased regime backtests.

6. **Skip the fancy stuff.** Aroon, Vortex, Choppiness Index, Fisher Transform, MESA, deep-learning regime classifiers, and sophisticated change-point methods (BOCPD, PELT) are mostly unjustified for a daily-bar retail system. They have weak empirical support beyond marketing posts, or their edge is dominated by simpler signals. The marginal complexity cost is real and the marginal Sharpe is negligible.

7. **The most important shift in mindset:** treat regime detection as a *risk-management and position-sizing layer*, not as a strategy on/off switch. Soft sizing on continuous regime confidence beats hard regime gating in nearly every study that compares them, mostly because hard gating concentrates whipsaw cost at the worst possible moments.

If you only do three things from this document: **VIX-aware position sizing, an HY OAS stress flag, and replacing hard regime filters with soft sizing weights.** Everything else is a nice-to-have.

## 1. Conceptual lay of the land

### 1.1 What "market regime" actually means in the literature

The canonical definition comes from James Hamilton's 1989 paper *"A New Approach to the Economic Analysis of Nonstationary Time Series and the Business Cycle"* ([Econometrica](https://www.jstor.org/stable/1912559)), which introduced Markov regime-switching models to economics. The core idea: a financial time series is generated by a small number of *latent states* (regimes), each with its own distributional parameters (mean, variance, sometimes higher moments), and you transition between them according to a Markov process.

Modern work descends from Hamilton in two main directions:

- **Latent-state econometrics**: Hidden Markov Models, regime-switching GARCH (Klaassen 2002, Marcucci 2005), Markov-switching VARs. Regimes are unobservable; you infer them from data using EM/Baum-Welch or Bayesian methods.
- **Cross-asset macro frameworks**: Bridgewater's growth/inflation quadrants ([All Weather](https://www.bridgewater.com/research-and-insights/the-all-weather-story)), AQR's risk-bucket dynamic asset allocation ([JPM 2014](https://www.aqr.com/-/media/AQR/Documents/Journal-Articles/JPM-Risk-Based-Dynamic-Asset-Allocation-with-Extreme-Tails-and-Correlations.pdf)), Goldman's bear-market indicator. Regimes are defined by macro variables (growth, inflation, credit, vol), not inferred from price alone.

The most-cited applied paper for equities is Ang & Bekaert (2002), *"International Asset Allocation with Regime Shifts"* ([Review of Financial Studies](https://academic.oup.com/rfs/article-abstract/15/4/1137/1582775)), which showed that equity correlations spike in bear regimes — meaning regime-aware portfolios beat static ones primarily by adapting to correlation breakdowns, not by directional timing.

### 1.2 Common taxonomies, ranked by how seriously practitioners take them

| Taxonomy | Source | Empirical support | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2-state HMM (low-vol / high-vol) | Hamilton tradition | Strong; replicated across decades | The default in modern quant work |
| 3-state HMM (calm / transitional / crisis) | Guidolin & Timmermann (2007) | Decent; "transitional" state is unstable | OK, but adds overfitting risk |
| Risk-on / risk-off | Cross-asset macro practitioners | Strong as a description; weaker as a forecastable signal | Useful as overlay, not primary classifier |
| High-vol / low-vol on VIX | VIX-based regime work | Very strong | Underrated; do this |
| Growth × Inflation quadrants | Bridgewater All Weather | Strong as allocation framework, weak as tactical signal | Excellent for asset allocation, less useful for SPY-only swing |
| Bull / bear / sideways | Folk technical analysis | Anecdotal; depends on definition | Fine as taxonomy; needs rigorous definition to be useful |
| **Trending / choppy / transitioning** | **Technical analysis (Wilder via ADX)** | **None academically; appears only in trader blogs** | **This is your current taxonomy. It's not how quants think.** |

The honest read: your current taxonomy is a *technical-analysis folk taxonomy*. It's not bad as an organizing principle — there are clearly markets that trend and markets that don't — but it's been operationalized via ADX+SMA, which is a shallow proxy. The serious practitioner taxonomy is **vol regime + credit/risk regime + (optional) trend regime**, with the first two doing most of the work.

### 1.3 The methodological landscape

There are basically four families of regime detection. Sorted from simplest to most complex:

1. **Rule-based composites** — combine indicators (vol, trend, breadth, credit) into a regime score, threshold or rank into states. Cheap, transparent, easy to debug. Most production retail systems live here whether they admit it or not.

2. **Latent-state models** — HMMs and regime-switching GARCH. Fit a generative model to (returns, vol, sometimes more), let the model discover states. Standard library: `hmmlearn` for HMMs, `arch` for GARCH.

3. **Change-point detection** — CUSUM, Bayesian Online Change-Point Detection (Adams & MacKay 2007), PELT (Killick et al. 2012). These tell you *when* regimes change but not *what* the new regime is. Library: `ruptures`.

4. **Supervised / unsupervised ML** — random forests, gradient boosting, neural nets, autoencoders. The literature here is large and unimpressive for retail; the regime signal is weak relative to the easy overfitting surface area of these models.

For a daily-bar retail system, **(1) and (2) are where the real ROI is**. (3) is a research curiosity. (4) is, with rare exceptions, a way to convert clean signal into noisy curve-fits.

## 2. Where ADX(14) + SMA(200) falls short

ADX + SMA isn't worthless — it captures the trend axis crudely and stops you from running mean-reversion strategies into a runaway uptrend or vice versa. But it has specific, well-known problems:

**(a) ADX is a smoothed double-derivative of price; it's lagging by construction.** ADX(14) computes Wilder-smoothed +DI/-DI from 14-day directional movement, then takes a moving average of their normalized difference. Empirically it lags trend changes by 5–15 bars on daily data — so by the time ADX confirms a trend, half the move has often happened, and by the time ADX confirms its end, the chop has already begun. This is fine if you're using ADX as an in-trend filter; it's bad if you're using it to *time* regime transitions.

**(b) ADX doesn't distinguish the two axes that actually matter.** A market can have rising ADX in a low-vol uptrend (great for trend strategies) or in a high-vol downtrend (terrible for any long strategy). ADX collapses these into a single number. Volatility is the missing axis.

**(c) SMA(200) is a single binary signal applied to a noisy series.** Whether SPY is above or below its 200d SMA is a useful long-term tape-reading shorthand, but the indicator whipsaws hard around the SMA in transitions (e.g., 2015, 2018 Q4, 2022). Slope of the SMA(200), or position relative to a band around it, is more robust than the raw cross.

**(d) Your `transitioning` state is doing a lot of unprincipled work.** From the system description, `transitioning` is essentially "ADX or SMA disagrees with the dominant trend signal." This means it's a bucket for ambiguous bars rather than a real regime. Strategies tagged for `transitioning` are being matched against a rule, not a state. There's no reason to think this state has any internal coherence — January 2008 transitions look nothing like March 2020 transitions.

**(e) No volatility awareness.** You can't tell the difference between a trending day with VIX=12 and a trending day with VIX=35. The risk of any swing strategy is enormously different in those two environments. ADX won't tell you.

**(f) No breadth or cross-asset awareness.** SPY can grind higher with terrible breadth (2024 — the Mag 7 carrying everything) or terrible credit (HY OAS widening before SPY rolls over). Your current classifier sees none of this.

**(g) No probability output.** ADX + SMA gives you a hard label. You can't size positions on confidence or fade signal weakness. Every regime-conditional decision is binary, which maximizes whipsaw at exactly the wrong moments.

The good news: each of these is fixable, and most fixes are cheap.

## 3. Indicator landscape: what works, what doesn't

This section is opinionated. I've grouped indicators by axis and tagged each with empirical-support level: **strong** (multiple academic papers + practitioner consensus), **medium** (academic but limited practitioner adoption, or vice versa), **weak** (mostly trader blogs, no rigorous backtest evidence).

### 3.1 Volatility regime (the most important axis)

- **VIX absolute level** — *strong*. Returns distributions, drawdown depth, and strategy Sharpe ratios all stratify cleanly by VIX bucket (typically <15, 15–25, >25). [VolatilityBox empirical analysis](https://volatilitybox.com/research/volatility-regimes-explained/), [Predicting Alpha](https://www.predictingalpha.com/implied-vs-realized-volatility/).
- **VIX vs. its own moving average (e.g., 200d)** — *strong*. VIX crossing above its 200d MA has preceded every major vol regime shift since 2005 (Feb 2018 Volmageddon, Mar 2020, 2022 bear). Detection lag 1–5 days for the simple crossover, 1–3 days for HMM, 0–2 days for ML — but the simpler signal is more robust.
- **VIX term structure (M1/M2 contango vs. backwardation)** — *medium*. Backwardation flags stress reliably; contango doesn't predict equity direction by itself. Useful as a stress overlay. [Macrosynergy on VIX term structure](https://macrosynergy.com/research/vix-term-structure-as-a-trading-signal/), [CBOE](https://www.cboe.com/insights/posts/inside-volatility-trading-is-vix-backwardation-necessarily-a-sign-of-a-future-down-market/).
- **Realized vol (20d/60d std of log returns)** — *strong*. Cheaper to compute than GARCH and almost as good for most regime work. The realized-implied vol spread (RV vs. VIX-derived implied) is informative but second-order.
- **GARCH(1,1) conditional vol** — *medium for retail*. Standard at derivatives desks; persistence coefficients of 0.8+ confirm vol clustering. For a retail daily-bar system, rolling realized vol captures most of the same signal at a fraction of the complexity. Worth knowing about; not worth implementing first.
- **VVIX (vol of vol)** — *medium*. Useful for detecting tail-risk repricing; spikes confirm panic. Hard to act on directly.

### 3.2 Trend strength (your current axis)

- **SMA slope (e.g., 200d, computed as ΔSMA over 20 days)** — *medium-strong*. Cleaner than the binary cross. Robust across timeframes. Used widely in CTA programs.
- **ADX(14)** — *medium*. Works as a "the trend has legs" filter. Lagging. Don't use in isolation.
- **Time-series momentum (Moskowitz, Ooi, Pedersen 2012)** — *strong*. Sign and magnitude of trailing 1–12 month returns, vol-scaled. This is the cleanest, most peer-reviewed trend signal. AQR even publishes [the original dataset](https://www.aqr.com/Insights/Datasets/Time-Series-Momentum-Original-Paper-Data). [SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2089463).
- **Hurst exponent (rolling, rescaled-range)** — *medium*. Distinguishes trending (H>0.5) from mean-reverting (H<0.5) regimes. Mathematically more rigorous than ADX. Some practitioner uptake. [Macrosynergy](https://macrosynergy.com/research/detecting-trends-and-mean-reversion-with-the-hurst-exponent/), [FractalCycles guide](https://fractalcycles.com/guides/rolling-hurst-exponent).
- **Variance ratio test (Lo–MacKinlay 1988)** — *medium*. Statistically rigorous test for random-walk vs. trend/mean-reversion. Underused vs. Hurst.
- **Aroon, Vortex, Choppiness Index** — *weak*. Show up endlessly in trader blogs, almost never in SSRN papers or hedge-fund disclosures. The Choppiness Index can serve as a filter ("don't trade trends when CHOP>61") but isn't predictive. Mostly noise.
- **Ehlers indicators (iTrend, Fisher Transform, MESA)** — *weak* in mainstream quant work, *medium* in a small Ehlers-disciple subculture. Mathematically interesting (signal-processing motivated) but not widely validated. Skip.

### 3.3 Breadth (the second axis you're missing)

- **% of S&P 500 stocks above 200d SMA** — *strong*. The simplest breadth measure and arguably the most robust. >80% = healthy uptrend; <20% = stress. Catches "narrow rally" regimes (2024 Mag 7) that price-only classifiers miss.
- **% above 50d SMA** — *strong*. Faster-moving cousin; better for swing horizons.
- **McClellan Oscillator and Summation Index** — *medium-strong*. Smoothed advances-minus-declines. Breadth thrust signals (sharp moves from oversold to strong positive readings) are well-documented bull-regime starts. [StockCharts](https://chartschool.stockcharts.com/table-of-contents/market-indicators/mcclellan-oscillator).
- **New highs vs. new lows** — *medium*. Divergence (price new high, declining new highs) is a classic exhaustion signal.
- **Composite breadth scores** — *strong*. Combining the above into a 0–100 score is widely used in production. No canonical formula; most implementations are simple averages of percentile-ranked components.

For a retail trader: ETF proxies make this cheaper. **RSP (equal-weight S&P)** vs. **SPY** captures concentration without needing constituent data. **The ratio of equal-weight to cap-weight indices is a one-line breadth proxy.** If you have constituents in EODHD, computing % above 50d/200d is straightforward but requires a constituent membership history (point-in-time, to avoid survivorship bias).

### 3.4 Cross-asset / macro (the axis you're definitely missing)

- **High-yield credit spreads (HY OAS, ICE BofA index `BAMLH0A0HYM2`)** — *strong*. Bond investors price for default risk on a fixed maturity, so HY spreads are leading-indicator gold. Widens 1–3 days before equity drawdowns reliably. Free via [FRED](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/BAMLH0A0HYM2). **If you only add one cross-asset signal, add this one.**
- **HY–IG spread differential** — *strong*. The gap between high-yield and investment-grade spreads tells you whether stress is broad (both widen) or specifically about lower-quality credit (HY widens, IG stable = early-stage stress).
- **Yield curve (2s10s, 3m10y)** — *medium for swing*. Excellent macro recession indicator with 6–24 month lead time. Too slow for swing-trading entries. Use as macro backdrop, not tactical signal. The 2022–2024 inversion has so far been a false positive, which is informative about the limits of this signal.
- **Dollar index (DXY)** — *medium*. DXY trend regime affects multinational earnings and EM/commodity correlations.
- **Defensive-vs-cyclical sector ratios (XLP/XLY, XLU/XLY)** — *medium*. A rising defensive ratio signals rotation into safety, often preceding broader weakness.
- **Bond-equity correlation regime** — *strong as a state descriptor, medium as a tactical signal*. Negative correlation = "growth regime" (rates and stocks move opposite); positive correlation = "inflation regime" (both suffer together). Massively important for portfolio risk; less obviously useful for SPY-only swing.
- **Gold/copper ratio, lumber/gold (Gundlach indicators)** — *weak*. Cute, frequently mentioned, weakly empirical.

### 3.5 Composite scores and dashboards

- **Goldman Sachs Bear Market Risk Indicator** — *medium*. Composite of unemployment, ISM, core inflation, yield-curve slope, Shiller PE. Score >70% historically warned of bears, but 2022–2024 was a false alarm. Useful macro backdrop, not tactical.
- **Citi Panic/Euphoria Model** — *medium*. Sentiment composite. Useful as a contrarian read.
- **State Street's ML regime work (2025)** — *medium*. Tree-based models on macro features, four-regime taxonomy (Expansion/Peak/Contraction/Trough). Interesting; not necessary for retail.
- **Quantpedia regime-switching strategies** — *medium*. Several backtested strategies; the database is paid but they publish summaries.

## 4. Methodological approaches: an opinionated ranking

### 4.1 Rule-based composite score (do this first)

A weighted regime score combining vol, trend, breadth, and credit is the boringly correct upgrade from your current system. Something like:

```
regime_score = (
    0.40 * vol_score        # 1 = low vol; 0 = high vol
  + 0.25 * trend_score      # 1 = strong uptrend; 0 = strong downtrend
  + 0.20 * breadth_score    # 1 = healthy breadth; 0 = narrow/weak
  + 0.15 * credit_score     # 1 = HY spreads tight; 0 = wide/widening
)
```

Each component is a percentile rank or a smoothed crossover indicator, scaled to 0–1. The aggregate score gets used as a continuous position-sizing multiplier, not a binary regime label. Strategies still declare regime affinity, but with soft weights:

- A trend-following strategy might get full size when `regime_score > 0.7` and zero size below 0.3, with a linear ramp in between.
- A mean-reversion strategy might prefer `regime_score` in 0.4–0.7 (moderate, choppy) and ramp down at the extremes.

This is unglamorous but it's what most successful retail/prosumer systems converge to. Transparent, debuggable, no look-ahead bias, no overfitting surface. **Expected improvement over ADX+SMA: +0.1 to +0.2 Sharpe, plus meaningful drawdown reduction.**

### 4.2 Hidden Markov Model (do this second)

A 2-state Gaussian HMM on `(log_return, realized_vol_20d)` is the right next step if you want a model-based classifier with probability outputs.

```python
from hmmlearn.hmm import GaussianHMM

X = np.column_stack([log_returns, realized_vol_20d])  # both standardized

model = GaussianHMM(n_components=2, covariance_type='diag', n_iter=200, random_state=42)
model.fit(X_train)

# Filtered (online) state probabilities for today
log_prob, posteriors = model.score_samples(X_train_plus_today)
today_prob = posteriors[-1]  # P(state | data through today)
```

**Critical implementation notes:**

1. **Walk-forward retraining.** Refit weekly or monthly on a rolling/expanding window. *Never* fit once on full history and use it for backtesting — that's look-ahead bias by construction. [QuantStart's HMM tutorial](https://www.quantstart.com/articles/market-regime-detection-using-hidden-markov-models-in-qstrader/), [QuantInsti regime-adaptive guide](https://blog.quantinsti.com/regime-adaptive-trading-python/).

2. **Use `score_samples` (filtered) for live classification, not `predict` on a full dataset.** The full-dataset Viterbi or `predict_proba` returns *smoothed* (forward-backward) posteriors that incorporate future data. Only valid for research, not trading.

3. **Relabel states by economic meaning each refit.** HMM state labels are arbitrary — state 0 might be "calm" in one fit and "stressed" in the next. After fitting, sort states by the mean of their return distribution (or by their volatility) and relabel consistently.

4. **Stick with 2 states.** 3-state models add a "transitional" state that is empirically unstable. The literature (Guidolin & Timmermann; Marcucci 2005) and practitioner experience converge on 2 states being the robust choice for daily equity data.

5. **Don't overload the observation vector.** `(return, vol)` is the canonical pair. Adding too many features doesn't help — HMMs are weak feature integrators and overfit easily on high-dimensional observations.

**Expected improvement over rule-based composite: +0.05 to +0.15 Sharpe, mostly through better-timed regime transitions.** The HMM is *not* magically better than rules; its main advantage is producing calibrated probabilities and adapting parameters automatically.

### 4.3 Change-point detection (skip)

BOCPD ([Adams & MacKay 2007](https://arxiv.org/abs/0710.3742)) and PELT (Killick et al. 2012, available in the [`ruptures`](https://centre-borelli.github.io/ruptures-docs/) library) are mathematically elegant. They detect *when* a regime changes but not *what* the new regime is. For your use case, that's the wrong abstraction — you want a continuous classifier, not a breakpoint detector.

There's a niche case: using BOCPD as a "regime-uncertainty" overlay that triggers position de-risking when the change-point hazard rate spikes. Cute, but not high-ROI for retail.

### 4.4 Machine learning classifiers (mostly skip)

Random forests, gradient boosting, and neural-net regime classifiers show up in dozens of recent papers and Medium posts. The honest assessment:

- The signal-to-noise ratio of regime classification is low.
- ML models have huge overfitting surface.
- On out-of-sample tests, they rarely beat well-specified HMMs or composite rules ([LSEG/Refinitiv comparison](https://developers.lseg.com/en/article-catalog/article/market-regime-detection)).
- They're black boxes, which is a real cost for a personal system you have to debug.

Skip unless you have a very specific hypothesis (e.g., "breadth thrusts predict regime transitions 5 days ahead"), in which case a *simple* logistic regression or shallow tree on a small feature set is fine. Don't grid-search 50 hyperparameters on 10 indicators.

### 4.5 GARCH and regime-switching GARCH (use selectively)

GARCH(1,1) in `arch` is the standard volatility forecaster in production at derivatives desks. For a daily-bar trader, rolling realized volatility captures most of the same information at a fraction of the complexity. Use GARCH if you want a forward-looking vol forecast (e.g., for sizing options trades or scaling risk to expected vol over the next 5 days). Don't use it as a regime classifier directly — it's a vol estimator, not a state-space model.

Regime-switching GARCH (Klaassen 2002, Marcucci 2005) is more rigorous than vanilla HMM for vol regimes specifically, but it's more complex and harder to fit stably. Not worth the marginal effort for a single-operator system.

## 5. Failure modes the literature warns about

These are the failure modes most commonly cited in the regime-detection literature, with direct implications for your system.

### 5.1 Look-ahead bias

This is the #1 reason "great" regime backtests don't survive contact with live trading.

- **Smoothed vs. filtered HMM posteriors.** Smoothed = uses future data; filtered = uses only past data. Trade only on filtered. Most introductory tutorials silently use smoothed and produce eye-popping backtests that don't replicate live.
- **Single-fit, full-history training.** Fitting an HMM (or any model) once on 2010–2025 and then "backtesting" 2010–2025 is look-ahead by construction. Walk-forward refitting is mandatory.
- **Threshold calibration on full history.** Z-score thresholds, percentile ranks, and "the VIX regime is high when VIX > 25" cutoffs derived from the entire backtest period embed future information. Calibrate on rolling windows.
- **Outcome-driven regime labels.** "This was a bull regime because returns were good" makes regime classification trivially correlated with returns. Define regimes from inputs (price, vol, breadth) only.

[López de Prado's *Advances in Financial Machine Learning*](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/Advances+in+Financial+Machine+Learning-p-9781119482086) is the canonical reference here; his Chapter 11 on backtest overfitting and his Combinatorially Symmetric Cross-Validation (CSCV) framework apply directly.

### 5.2 Regime instability and non-stationarity

Markets are non-stationary. The 2022 inflation regime had no analog in the prior 30 years of training data. The 2020 COVID regime was a sui generis liquidity event. Any regime model fit on pre-2020 data was essentially flying blind in March 2020.

Practical implications:

- **Refit periodically.** Quarterly is a reasonable default. Refit immediately after any 3σ+ move.
- **Don't trust regime-conditional Sharpe ratios from a single backtest period.** Test across at least one crisis (2008, 2020) and one extended trend (2010–2019, 2017–2021).
- **Hyperparameter sensitivity is real.** A 2-state HMM that works in one decade may need to be a 3-state model in another. Resist the temptation to optimize this.

### 5.3 Overfitting

The combinatorial space of regime definitions is enormous: 5 indicators × 4 lookbacks × 3 thresholds = 60 combinations, before you even get to weights. Grid-searching this on a single backtest is a guaranteed overfit.

- **Pre-specify before backtesting.** Decide your regime definition first; backtest second.
- **Use information criteria (BIC/AIC) for state-count selection in HMMs**, not Sharpe ratio.
- **Reserve a holdout period that's never seen during model selection.**
- **Use CSCV or purged k-fold cross-validation** if you're going to do any model search.

[Bailey & López de Prado (2014), "The Probability of Backtest Overfitting"](https://www.davidhbailey.com/dhbpapers/backtest-prob.pdf), is the foundational paper here.

### 5.4 Whipsaw and transition cost

Naive regime classifiers flip 15–25 times per year on daily SPY data. Each flip has a real transaction cost (commissions, spread, slippage) that compounds.

- **Add hysteresis.** Require N consecutive bars in the new regime before switching (N=2–5).
- **Use soft sizing instead of hard gating.** A regime confidence of 0.55 should mean "size at 55%," not "fully on" or "fully off."
- **Measure regime-flip frequency and transaction-cost drag explicitly in your backtests.**

### 5.5 The "regimes are a story we tell ourselves" critique

The strongest skeptical argument: regimes are post-hoc narrative fitting. A 2-state HMM will always find two clusters in any time series; that doesn't mean those clusters are tradeable.

The honest counterpoint: vol regimes are *empirically real and persistent* (vol clusters; this is the most replicated stylized fact in financial econometrics, going back to Mandelbrot 1963). Credit/risk regimes are real (HY spreads have a clear regime structure tied to the credit cycle). Pure return regimes (bull vs. bear) are real but slower and less tradeable on swing horizons.

Practical implication: **anchor your regime classifier in vol and credit**, where the signal is robust. Be skeptical of regimes defined purely on price/return features.

### 5.6 HMM identifiability and label-switching

EM/Baum-Welch fits of HMMs have local optima and arbitrary state labels. Two consecutive refits can produce models that label "calm" and "stressed" in opposite orders. This breaks any code that relies on `state == 0` meaning the same thing across refits.

Fix: after each refit, reorder states by an economic criterion (mean of the return distribution, or the variance of the volatility distribution) and relabel consistently.

## 6. Prioritized upgrade path for your system

Concrete, ranked by effort/payoff.

### Tier 0 — Don't do anything before you've done these

**(a) Add a VIX-aware position-sizing layer.** Pull VIX (free from FRED `VIXCLS` or Yahoo). Compute a vol-regime score:

```
vol_score = clip( (VIX_threshold_high - VIX) / (VIX_threshold_high - VIX_threshold_low), 0, 1)
# e.g., thresholds 15 and 30: VIX=15 -> 1.0, VIX=30 -> 0.0, VIX=22.5 -> 0.5
```

Multiply *every* strategy's position size by `vol_score`. Or, more conservatively, by `0.5 + 0.5*vol_score` to keep some exposure on at all times.

**Effort: 1–2 hours. Expected impact: meaningful drawdown reduction, modest Sharpe lift, no downside.**

**(b) Add a credit-stress flag.** Pull HY OAS from FRED (`BAMLH0A0HYM2`). Compute the 30-day percentile rank of HY OAS within the trailing 1-year window. When HY OAS >85th percentile *and rising over the past 5 days*, flag a credit-stress regime.

In credit-stress regime: cut all long position sizes by 50%, skip new long entries, tighten stops. This is a tail-risk circuit breaker, not a primary signal.

**Effort: 1–2 hours. Expected impact: protects against the worst drawdowns, which is where regime detection earns its keep.**

**(c) Replace hard regime filters with soft sizing.** Compute a continuous regime score (Tier 1 below) and use it to scale position size, not to gate strategies on/off. Strategies still declare affinity, but as preferences (e.g., "prefer vol_score > 0.6"), implemented as smooth weighting rather than binary filters.

**Effort: a few hours, mostly refactoring. Expected impact: less whipsaw cost, smoother equity curve, easier to debug.**

### Tier 1 — Build a proper rule-based regime composite

Replace `trending_up / trending_down / choppy / transitioning` with a continuous **regime health score** in [0, 1], built from:

- **Vol component (40%):** percentile rank of VIX (inverted), or 1 - (realized_vol_20d / realized_vol_252d_max).
- **Trend component (25%):** sign and magnitude of SMA(200) slope over 20 days, plus position relative to SMA(200).
- **Breadth component (20%):** RSP/SPY ratio trend (cheap proxy) or % stocks above 50d SMA (if you have constituents).
- **Credit component (15%):** percentile rank of HY OAS (inverted).

Each component standardized to 0–1; weighted average gives a regime health score. Threshold this into 3 buckets if you really need labels (e.g., 0.66+ = healthy, 0.33–0.66 = neutral, <0.33 = stressed), but prefer using the raw continuous score.

**Effort: a weekend. Expected impact: this is the actual upgrade. Most of the value of "regime detection" for a retail system lives at this tier.**

### Tier 2 — Add a 2-state HMM

Once your rule-based composite is working, add a parallel 2-state Gaussian HMM on `(log_return, realized_vol_20d)`. Use it to produce a posterior probability of the "calm" state.

- Walk-forward refit weekly on a 3-year rolling window.
- Use only filtered (online) probabilities at decision time.
- Relabel states by mean return after each refit.
- Use HMM probability as either (a) a fifth component of the composite score, or (b) a sanity-check signal that diverges from the rule-based composite.

**Effort: a few days including walk-forward backtesting infrastructure. Expected impact: modest. The HMM mostly replicates the composite score with marginally smoother transitions and calibrated probabilities.**

### Tier 3 — Optional enhancements

Order them however appeals:

- **Hurst exponent (rolling, 100-bar window)** as a trend-vs-mean-reversion classifier. Useful if you run mean-reversion strategies and want to gate them on "currently mean-reverting" regimes.
- **Term-structure stress flag** from VIX/VIX3M ratio (or VIX vs. VXV). Backwardation as additional stress signal.
- **Bond-equity correlation regime** (rolling 20d corr of SPY and TLT). Positive correlation = inflation regime, sizing implications for any leverage.
- **Defensive sector ratio** (XLP/XLY). Rising = defensive rotation = caution.

None of these are critical. Each adds a small amount of marginal information and a small amount of overfitting surface.

### Tier 4 — Skip these unless you have a specific reason

- BOCPD/PELT change-point detection
- Regime-switching GARCH
- ML/deep-learning regime classifiers
- Hudson & Thames `mlfinlab` (overkill and expensive)
- 4+ state HMMs
- Aroon, Vortex, Choppiness Index, Fisher Transform, MESA, Ehlers indicators

These are the things that most retail quant content gets excited about. They're mostly low-ROI for a personal daily-bar SPY system.

## 7. How to backtest regime-conditional strategies properly

Brief but important. Most regime backtests are subtly broken.

1. **Walk-forward retraining of the regime model.** Refit every N bars (weekly, monthly) on a rolling window. Classify out-of-sample bars using only the model fit on prior data.

2. **Use filtered (online) probabilities only.** Never use smoothed posteriors for decisions.

3. **Relabel HMM states each refit** by economic criterion to keep state 0 = "calm" stable across refits.

4. **Realistic transaction costs.** 1–5 bps per side on SPY; more if you're trading individual names. Run the backtest with and without costs to see how much regime-flip whipsaw is eating you.

5. **Per-regime return analysis.** Decompose strategy returns by regime to verify the strategy is doing what you think in each regime. A momentum strategy should outperform in trending regimes; if it doesn't, your regime classifier is mislabeled or your strategy is misspecified.

6. **Test across multiple market cycles.** 2008, 2011, 2015, 2018 Q4, 2020, 2022 are the obvious stress tests. If a regime-conditional strategy beats buy-and-hold only in a single decade, it's overfit.

7. **Compare against a vol-scaled buy-and-hold benchmark.** Many "regime-conditional" strategies are just doing vol scaling in disguise. If your regime model adds nothing beyond `1/realized_vol` position sizing, you've overcomplicated the problem.

8. **CSCV / purged cross-validation** (López de Prado). If you've done any meaningful model selection, you owe yourself a backtest-overfitting probability estimate.

## 8. Recommended Python stack

- **`pandas`, `numpy`, `scipy`** — non-negotiable.
- **`pandas-ta`** — pre-built indicators. Don't reinvent ADX.
- **`hmmlearn`** — only practical HMM library for Python. BSD license.
- **`arch`** — GARCH if you decide you want it.
- **`ruptures`** — change-point detection if you decide you want it. (Probably you don't.)
- **`vectorbt`** — fast vectorized backtesting; can run a 10-year regime-conditional backtest in seconds. Free tier is fine.
- **`yfinance` or `pandas-datareader`** — free FRED and Yahoo data. Useful for VIX, HY OAS, TLT, GLD, DXY.
- **EODHD** for OHLCV and constituents.

You don't need anything else. Resist the urge to install `mlfinlab`, TensorFlow, or any deep-learning framework for this problem.

## 9. The honest summary

Your current ADX(14) + SMA(200) + 4-regime taxonomy isn't *wrong* — it captures the trend axis crudely and prevents the most obvious mistakes (running a long-only momentum strategy into a 2008-style downtrend, or a mean-reversion strategy into a 2020 vertical drop). But it's missing the two axes that do most of the actual work in modern regime literature: **volatility** and **credit/risk stress**. And it's using a hard-label taxonomy where soft, continuous regime confidence would be more useful for sizing.

The high-ROI upgrades, in order:

1. **VIX-aware position sizing** (1 day of work, large impact).
2. **HY OAS credit-stress flag** (half a day, tail-risk insurance).
3. **Switch from hard regime gates to soft sizing weights** (refactor, modest effort).
4. **Build a rule-based 4-component regime composite** (weekend, this is the real upgrade).
5. **Add a 2-state HMM as a parallel signal** (few days, modest marginal value).

Everything else (Hurst, change-point, ML, GARCH, multi-state HMMs, fancy breadth dashboards) is optional polish or research curiosity. Don't get distracted by it before you've nailed the basics.

The single biggest mindset shift: **regime detection is mostly a position-sizing and risk-management tool, not a signal-generating tool.** Treat it that way and your system will be significantly more robust. Treat it as a magic timing oracle and you'll spend years curve-fitting and never quite trusting the output.
